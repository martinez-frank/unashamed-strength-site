About
Faith-first training apparel for the ones who refuse to be defined by fear, failure, or the past.

I was diagnosed with cancer ten years ago, when I was thirty years old.

What people don’t know is that when I first found out, I didn’t tell anyone. I was embarrassed. I was scared. I was nervous. I had no idea what was going to happen to my life. I didn’t know how bad it was, and I didn’t know how to even say the words out loud.

So I kept it to myself.

For almost two years — maybe longer — I carried that weight alone. I went on with life like nothing was wrong, even though everything was. Looking back, I realize how much fear controlled me during that time. I was young. I was healthy. I never once thought cancer would be part of my story. Not even for a second.

The only person I finally told was my older brother.

When I told him, I was still embarrassed. Still scared. Still unsure of what was coming. I remember not knowing if I wanted answers or if I was just hoping it would somehow go away. But my brother didn’t sugarcoat anything. He told me something I needed to hear, not something that was easy to hear.

He told me I had no choice — I had to go get checked before I died.

So I went to the ER.

And that’s when everything changed.

I found out I had Stage IV cancer.

In a blink of an eye, my entire life stopped. I didn’t know if I was going to live or not. I didn’t know how much time I had left. I didn’t know what was coming next. All I knew was that the future I thought I had was suddenly uncertain.

What made it even harder was this — I was healthy. I worked out. I took care of myself. I never imagined in my mind that I would have this disease. Cancer wasn’t something I associated with my life, my age, or my body. But there it was, staring me in the face.

Those were the darkest moments of my life.

But I didn’t go through them alone.

My brother stood by me. God carried me. Prayer surrounded me — even when I didn’t have the strength to pray for myself. There were moments when faith was the only thing holding me together. Moments when all I could do was surrender and trust that there was a reason I was still breathing.

I didn’t survive by accident. I’m still here today for a reason — to impact lives, to tell the truth, to spread the Word, to follow Christ, and to do the right thing even when it’s hard.

Romans 11:6

Looking back now, I know I didn’t survive by accident.

Cancer didn’t just change my body — it changed my direction. It stripped me down and showed me what really matters.

I didn’t survive to stay silent.

I survived to live unashamed, to walk in faith, and to remind others that even when life tries to end you, God may still be getting ready to use you.
